01 · Foundations

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals impersonate a trusted person, brand, or organization — like your bank, a delivery company, or even your boss — to trick you into handing over sensitive information such as passwords, OTPs, credit card numbers, or to get you to install malware.

The name is a play on "fishing": attackers cast out bait (a fake email, message, or website) and wait for someone to bite. Unlike hacking a system directly, phishing hacks a person — it relies on psychology, not just technology.

Phishing can arrive through email, text messages, phone calls, social media, or fake websites, and it is the starting point for the vast majority of data breaches and ransomware attacks worldwide.

Why it works

  • It exploits trust in familiar brands and people.
  • It creates urgency so victims act before thinking.
  • It looks convincingly real — logos, tone, formatting.
  • It targets emotion (fear, greed, curiosity) over logic.

02 · Attack Vectors

Types of Phishing

📧

Email Phishing

Mass emails sent to thousands of people, pretending to be a bank, retailer, or service provider, asking you to click a link or open an attachment.

🎯

Spear Phishing

A targeted attack aimed at one person or organization, using personal details (name, role, company) to appear highly credible and specific.

📱

Smishing (SMS)

Phishing via text message — often fake delivery alerts, prize notifications, or bank warnings with a malicious link.

☎️

Vishing (Voice)

Phone call scams where attackers impersonate bank staff, tech support, or government officials to extract information verbally.

03 · Detection

How to Recognize Phishing Emails

🚩 Red Flags to Watch For

  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your real name.
  • Sender address that looks close, but not exact — e.g. support@paypa1.com.
  • Urgent or threatening language: "act now", "account suspended".
  • Spelling, grammar, or formatting mistakes.
  • Unexpected attachments or links you weren't expecting.
  • Requests for passwords, OTPs, or payment details via email.
  • Mismatched or suspicious links — hover before you click.

📩 Example: Annotated Phishing Email

From: Amaz0n-Rewards@delivery-notice.info Fake domain
Subject: Congratulations! Claim your $500 gift card Too good to be true
Dear Valued Customer, Generic greeting

You have been randomly selected. Click here within 12 hours Urgency to claim your reward.

04 · Detection

How to Identify Fake Websites

🔗 URL Tricks

Attackers use lookalike domains such as paypa1.com, netfl1x-billing.com, or add extra words like bank-login-secure.com. Always read the URL carefully, especially right before the first single "/".

🔒 HTTPS Isn't Everything

A padlock icon only means the connection is encrypted — it does not mean the site is legitimate. Scammers can get HTTPS certificates too. Always verify the domain name itself.

🎨 Design & Content Issues

Look for low-quality logos, broken layouts, outdated copyright years, missing "Contact Us" or "About" pages, and pop-ups demanding immediate login.

05 · Psychology

Social Engineering Tactics

⏰

Urgency

"Act within 24 hours or your account will be closed." Pressure stops people from thinking clearly.

😨

Fear

"Suspicious login detected." Fear of loss or punishment pushes victims to comply immediately.

🤝

Trust

Impersonating a known brand, coworker, or authority figure to lower your guard.

💰

Greed

"You've won a prize!" Promises of free money or rewards exploit desire for quick gain.

06 · Defense

Prevention Tips

  • Use strong, unique passwords for every account — consider a password manager.
  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) wherever possible.
  • Never click links or open attachments from unexpected messages.
  • Type known website addresses directly instead of clicking links.
  • Verify requests for money or information through a separate channel (call the company directly).
  • Keep software, browsers, and antivirus tools up to date.
  • Report suspicious emails to your IT/security team instead of ignoring them.

Remember:

No legitimate bank, company, or government agency will ever ask you to share your password or OTP over email, text, or phone. When in doubt, pause, verify independently, and never let urgency make the decision for you.

💡 Golden Rule: If a message creates panic and demands instant action — stop. That reaction is the attack.

07 · Case Studies

Real-World Phishing Examples

🏦 Fake Bank Alert

Victims receive an SMS/email claiming their bank account is "blocked" and must be "reactivated" by entering login details on a fake bank-lookalike page.

🔑 Fake OTP Request

A caller pretends to be from your bank and asks you to "confirm" the OTP you just received to "cancel a fraudulent transaction" — the OTP is then used to steal your money.

🎁 Lottery / Prize Scam

A message claims you've won a lottery or lucky draw you never entered, asking for a "processing fee" or personal details to release the prize.
